# Analytical Thinking (T)

## Technical Definition
Analytical Thinking (based on Pennebaker's LIWC research) measures the degree to which a person uses logical, formal, and hierarchical language. High scores reflect structured, categorized reasoning, while low scores indicate a more intuitive, narrative, and "here-and-now" style.

## Prompt Engineering Guidelines
When evaluating for Analytical Thinking, look for:
- **High T:** Use of articles (a, an, the) and prepositions. Complex sentence structures with logical connectors (because, therefore, although). Focus on objects and categories.
- **Low T:** Use of pronouns (I, we, they), auxiliary verbs, and informal narrative markers. Focus on personal stories and immediate experiences.

**Evaluation Prompt snippet:**
"Rate the text from 0.0 to 1.0 on Analytical Thinking (T). 1.0 is a formal, categorized, and logical analysis. 0.0 is a personal, narrative, or intuitive stream of consciousness."

## Evaluation Rubric
- **0.00 (Narrative/Personal):** Purely stream-of-consciousness, story-focused, heavy use of emotional markers ("I felt", "I saw"), lacks logical hierarchy.
- **0.25 (Conversational):** Primarily personal experience; some light logical framing but structure is secondary.
- **0.50 (Balanced):** Mixes personal perspective with some causal logic.
- **0.75 (Analytical):** Demonstrates structure; clear usage of causal connectives ("therefore", "consequently").
- **1.00 (Systematic/Formal):** Deeply hierarchical, objective, uses formal/technical definitions, devoid of narrative markers.

## Scoring Examples
- **1.0 (High):** "The atmospheric CO2 levels correlate directly with the industrial output of the tertiary sector, necessitating a structural shift in energy policy."
- **0.5 (Neutral):** "I think the environment is changing because of what we do at work, so we should probably look at new ways to get power."
- **0.0 (Low):** "I felt so sad seeing the smoke today. It reminded me of my childhood and how I just want things to be green again."
